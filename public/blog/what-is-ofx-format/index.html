What is OFX Format? A Complete Guide

Published March 2026 · 4 min read

OFX stands for Open Financial Exchange. It is a data specification for the electronic exchange of financial information between financial institutions, businesses, and consumers. Developed jointly by CheckFree, Intuit, and Microsoft in 1997, OFX has become the standard format used worldwide for importing bank transactions into personal finance and accounting software.

Why OFX Matters

When you download your bank statement, you typically get a PDF — which looks great for reading but is useless for software. OFX is machine-readable: it contains structured transaction data that apps can directly import, categorise, and analyse. No manual typing, no spreadsheet wrangling.

Which Apps Support OFX?

OFX is supported by virtually every major personal finance application:

  • Quicken (full OFX support since 1997)
  • YNAB (You Need A Budget)
  • GnuCash (free and open source)
  • Banktivity (macOS/iOS)
  • Moneydance
  • HomeBank (free, cross-platform)
  • Microsoft Money (legacy)

What Does an OFX File Look Like?

OFX files use a tag-based structure similar to XML. Each transaction is enclosed in STMTTRN tags and includes the date, amount, type (DEBIT or CREDIT), and a description. The file also includes account information such as bank ID, account number, and currency.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is OFX the same as QFX?

QFX is a Quicken-proprietary variant of OFX. They are nearly identical in structure; most apps that accept OFX also accept QFX.

Can I open an OFX file in Excel?

Not directly, but you can rename it to .txt and open it to inspect the contents. For proper analysis, import it into a finance app first.
